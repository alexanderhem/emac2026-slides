Background
03 / 13
Two systems now operating in grocery stores.
Type A
Human-designed algorithm
Rule-based. Designed and actively monitored by staff. The pricing logic is set by people — and people continue to oversee it. Price adjustments are deliberate, traceable, and subject to human review at any point.
Traceable · Accountable · Interpretable
Type B
Self-learning AI
Autonomous. Learns and updates without human oversight. Prices adapt based on complex patterns in large datasets — without anyone reviewing each individual decision. The system's internal logic is difficult to inspect or explain.
Opaque · Autonomous · Black box
Research Question
Does the source of a pricing algorithm change how fair the price feels — and if so, why?
Theory
04 / 13
Why the source should matter
01
Price fairness is procedural
Fairness reflects not just what you pay, but how the price was decided. When the pricing process is opaque or difficult to follow, procedural unfairness dominates the consumer's evaluation — regardless of the price level itself.
Campbell (1999); Ferguson, Ellen & Bearden (2014); Bolton, Warlop & Alba (2003)
02
Two generations of algorithms — two levels of opacity
Human-designed, rule-based systems are traceable and accountable — consumers can, in principle, follow the logic. Self-learning AI adjusts prices autonomously through complex data patterns, making its workings difficult to interpret. This opacity is the source of the fairness penalty.
Calvano et al. (2019); Seele, Dierksmeier, Hofstetter & Schultz (2021)
03
Transparency is the mechanism
When consumers cannot understand how a price was set, perceived fairness drops. It is not algorithm type per se that matters — it is what consumers can understand about it. Transparency bridges the source of the price and the judgment of its fairness.
Rothenberger (2015); procedural justice  →  H4
Theory
05 / 13
Not all consumers feel the difference equally
Moderator 1 — H2
Technology Anxiety
Fear and apprehension about computer technology. Consumers high in tech anxiety are less confident in complex data-driven systems and more likely to perceive autonomous AI pricing as opaque and potentially manipulative — amplifying the fairness gap.
H2
Tech anxiety amplifies the fairness advantage of human-designed pricing over AI.
Meuter et al. (2003, 2005) · Kang, Kim & Lakshmanan (2024)
Moderator 2 — H3
Wastefulness with Money
Consumers who are careful with money — tightwads — are more attuned to how prices are set. They are more likely to scrutinize who or what decided the price. Spendthrifts, less focused on cost implications, are less sensitive to the pricing mechanism.
H3
Wastefulness reduces the fairness advantage of human-designed pricing over AI.
Rick, Cryder & Loewenstein (2008)

10 / 13
H2 & H3: Moderation — an honest read
H2 — Technology Anxiety  ·  Moderation not established
A conditional pattern — but no reliable moderation
The interaction is nonsignificant (b = −0.09, p = .492). A Johnson–Neyman probe flags a mid-range band (2.53–3.88) where the AI penalty is distinguishable from zero — but the conditional effect is monotonic, in fact largest at high anxiety. The band reflects where estimates are most precise, not a substantive cutoff.
We report the band descriptively and do not claim that technology anxiety moderates the effect.
H3 — Wastefulness with Money  ·  Moderation not established
A conditional pattern — but no reliable moderation
The interaction is nonsignificant (p = .080) — though closer to threshold than H2. A conditional effect emerges for low-wastefulness consumers (b = −0.63, p = .025), the price-attentive group, but the interaction does not clear conventional thresholds.
On the interaction evidence, wastefulness is the stronger of the two candidates — the reverse of the original grading. We claim neither.

Discussion
11 / 13
What the results tell us
01
AI pricing is less fair — because it is less interpretable
The fairness penalty is not about the price level — it is procedural. AI systems are perceived as harder to understand, and that opacity drives the unfairness judgment. This is exactly what procedural justice theory predicts.
02
Transparency is the mechanism — and the theoretical contribution
The mediation finding extends procedural justice theory to algorithmic pricing: it is not the algorithm type per se that drives unfairness, but what consumers can understand about it. Prior work shows effects; this study identifies the pathway.
03
Individual differences: a boundary, not yet established
Neither moderator's interaction reached significance — technology anxiety (p = .492) or wastefulness (p = .080). Conditional probes hint the gap is sharper for the most price-attentive consumers, but we treat both as boundary explorations for future work, not as established effects.
Implications
12 / 13
What this means for retailers
If transparency is the mechanism, transparency is also the lever. Retailers can't easily change that AI feels less fair — but they can change how legible the pricing process is.
The lever
Make the pricing process legible
The penalty is procedural — it comes from opacity, not the price level. Clear explanations and transparency cues on digital shelf labels target the actual cause. A simple "why this price" rationale can recover perceived fairness for AI-set prices.
In practice
Design the cue, not just the system
Plain-language pricing explanations, price-history displays, and QR codes linking to the logic behind an adjustment. What closes the gap is consumer comprehension — not exposing the algorithm's technical detail.
The limit
Transparency has a ceiling
Machine-learning pricing models are partial black boxes; full interpretability may be infeasible. Signaling human oversight — or a hybrid human-AI framing — can convey accountability where complete disclosure cannot.
Who to prioritize
The fairness gap is sharpest among shoppers with moderate technology anxiety and low wastefulness — the most price- and process-attentive segments.

B9
Where this research goes next
→
Hybrid pricing — a 2×2 design
Our conditions bundle two factors: logic source and human oversight. A 2×2 (human-designed vs. AI-derived × monitored vs. autonomous), with transparency as mediator, un-confounds them — and tests whether a human-in-the-loop signal alone restores fairness over an AI black box.
→
Cross-cultural replication
The US sample is a starting point. EU consumers face a distinct regulatory context (AI Act, right to explanation). Algorithmic trust varies substantially across cultures and regulatory environments.
→
Behavioral consequences
Perceived unfairness should predict store switching, reduced purchase likelihood, and negative word-of-mouth. Moving from perception to behavior is the field's essential next step.
→
Transparency interventions
If transparency is the mechanism, what specific disclosures close the fairness gap? Price history displays, QR explanations, shelf label reasoning — empirically testing what works has direct retailer value.

What "AI pricing" meant operationally
Human Condition — Full Stimulus Text
Many grocery stores are shifting toward digital shelf labels that allow prices to be updated electronically. Prices for individual products can be updated several times per day in response to supplier costs, inventory levels, shopper demand, competition, and promotions.

How the pricing algorithm works: Prices are set by an algorithm designed by humans and actively monitored by the store's pricing team. Human staff follow the system's performance and make updates or improvements when needed.
AI Condition — Full Stimulus Text
[Identical context paragraph]

How the pricing algorithm works: Prices are set by an algorithm developed by artificial intelligence (AI), which learns and updates itself without human monitoring. The system adapts its pricing approach independently over time.
→ The operationalized axis
Human oversight + rule-based vs. autonomous + self-learning. Both are "algorithmic." Consumer perception of this distinction — not the technical classification — is what drives the fairness effect. This motivates a three-condition follow-up: human / hybrid / fully autonomous AI.

B2
On p = .080 for H3 (Wastefulness)
Why we don't claim support
The conventional α = .05 threshold exists precisely to limit Type I errors. p = .080 does not constitute reliable evidence of moderation. Reporting it as "marginally significant" would overstate the finding — reviewers correctly flagged this risk.
What the conditional effects show
Low-wastefulness consumers: b = −0.63, p = .025. The effect is real for price-sensitive consumers — but the interaction term (the correct test for moderation) does not support H3. We report both without conflating them.
→ Implication
The finding motivates a better-powered follow-up with a larger sample and sharper operationalization of the wastefulness / immediate-reward-seeking distinction flagged by reviewers.

B3
Probing the moderators: consistency across H2 and H3
Both interactions are nonsignificant
Technology anxiety (p = .492) and wastefulness (p = .080) both fail to reach significance — so neither establishes moderation. On the interaction evidence, wastefulness is in fact the closer of the two. We were inconsistent to grade them differently, and we now report both symmetrically: suggestive, not supported.
What the conditional probes do — and don't — show
Johnson–Neyman (H2) and simple slopes (H3) describe where a conditional effect is distinguishable from zero; they are not tests of moderation. The H2 JN band (2.53–3.88) is precision-bounded — the effect is monotonic, largest at high anxiety, so the band marks where standard errors are smallest, not a real cutoff. The H3 effect is significant only at low wastefulness.
→ Implication
Future work should pre-register one probing rule applied uniformly across moderators, and power the design for the interaction term itself rather than the conditional slopes.

B5
External validity — what we can and cannot claim
What the design supports
The experiment establishes a causal relationship: perception of AI vs. human pricing causally affects fairness judgments, with transparency as the mechanism. The internal validity of the causal inference is sound.
Known constraints
US-centric sample · Hypothetical scenario (no real purchase) · Self-reported fairness, not behavioral outcomes (store switching, purchase likelihood) · Single retail format (grocery DSL)
Why the US sample is defensible
Digital shelf label adoption is highest in the US and Northern Europe. US consumers are actively encountering real-time algorithmic pricing. The scenario reflects current retail practice, not a hypothetical future.
The motivated next step
European sample (EU AI Act context, right to explanation) · Real purchase behavior as DV · Field experiment in a live DSL retail environment. These are the studies that establish generalizability.

B6
Hybrid pricing — and why the next study is a 2×2
What our two conditions actually varied
Our conditions differ on two things at once: who designs the pricing logic (human rules vs. AI-derived patterns) and whether a human stays in the loop (monitored vs. autonomous). So "human" is really designed + supervised, and "AI" is AI-derived + autonomous. A hybrid — AI logic with human oversight — is the cell we did not test.
The decisive next study: a 2×2
Cross logic source (human-designed vs. AI-derived) with oversight (monitored vs. autonomous), measuring transparency as the mediator. This un-confounds the two factors and isolates which facet of transparency drives fairness: being able to follow the logic, or knowing a human is accountable.
→ Why it matters
If a credible human-in-the-loop signal alone restores fairness — even over an opaque AI — retailers could close the gap without making the algorithm itself interpretable. That is a far lighter intervention than full explainability, and it is the practical question the 2×2 is built to answer.

Why this is acceptable
The manipulation check tests differential leakage — whether one condition produced systematically better understanding. It did not (p = .218). Absolute accuracy ~65% is within normal range for complex experimental vignettes.
Sensitivity analysis
Restricted to participants who correctly identified the pricing algorithm: Δ = −0.417, 95% CI [−0.800, −0.035], p = .033. The effect strengthens. The finding is not driven by misunderstanding — it is driven by accurate perception of the manipulation.
